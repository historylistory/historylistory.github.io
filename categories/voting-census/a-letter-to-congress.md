### SAMPLE LETTER

Dear Members of Congress:

The United States is facing a sustained and escalating assault on free and fair elections. These events cannot be dismissed as isolated controversies. Together, they reveal a coordinated pattern of election denial, voter intimidation, partisan manipulation, racial disenfranchisement, and federal interference in state election administration.

For years, Donald Trump and his allies have conditioned supporters to reject any election they do not win. Trump called the 2012 election a “total sham,” claimed before both the 2016 and 2020 elections that the results would be rigged, pressured Georgia officials to “find” enough votes to reverse his defeat, and encouraged efforts to obstruct certification of the 2020 election. Even after courts and officials found no evidence sufficient to overturn the result, his allies continued spreading claims that exposed election workers such as Ruby Freeman and Shaye Moss to racist threats and threats of execution.

This campaign of disinformation has been accompanied by direct intimidation. Trump has repeatedly threatened to deploy law-enforcement officers or uniformed federal agents near polling places. Political operatives have used deceptive robocalls to suppress Black voters. More recently, administration officials and allies have discussed involving ICE in election operations, obtaining state voter files, seizing election records and equipment, and threatening election officials with criminal prosecution.

At the same time, partisan officials are manipulating district boundaries to predetermine election outcomes. Mid-decade redistricting efforts have targeted majority-Black districts, divided predominantly Black cities among Republican-leaning districts, and attempted to eliminate opposition seats at the request of the president. In several states, maps were advanced through emergency sessions, suspended elections, and rushed votes—including while residents were evacuating during a tornado warning. This is not representative government. It is the deliberate selection of voters by politicians rather than the selection of politicians by voters.

Voting access itself is also under attack. Efforts to restrict mail voting, impose burdensome citizenship-document requirements, centralize voter information, purge voter rolls, and weaken the Voting Rights Act disproportionately threaten married women, Native Americans, Black voters, naturalized citizens, elderly voters, and people without ready access to passports or original records. Meanwhile, federal agencies have been used to demand ballots, voter-registration records, citizenship data, and other sensitive information from states.

Congress has a constitutional duty to act. It must restore and strengthen the Voting Rights Act, prohibit partisan mid-decade gerrymandering, protect election workers, restrict the use of military or federal law-enforcement personnel at polling locations, safeguard mail voting, protect voter data, and establish meaningful penalties for officials who knowingly attempt to overturn lawful election results.

Silence is not neutrality. Delay is not caution. When political leaders openly discuss taking voting rights away, terminating constitutional protections, using federal force around elections, or eliminating elections altogether, Congress must respond with more than statements of concern.

The right to vote is not a privilege granted by a president, a political party, a billionaire, a pastor, or a state legislature. It belongs to the people. Congress must defend it before the machinery of democratic government is placed entirely under partisan control.
