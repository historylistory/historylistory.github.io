### SAMPLE LETTER

Dear Sir or Madam,

I am deeply concerned about the state of our economy and the direction in which our country is headed. These concerns are not limited to inflation, wages, housing costs, or job availability. They extend to a broader pattern of economic mismanagement, corporate consolidation, conflicts of interest, and a growing disconnect between political leadership and the daily realities facing ordinary Americans.

Millions of Americans are struggling to make ends meet. Parents are skipping meals so their children can eat. Workers are juggling multiple jobs while falling further behind. Yet instead of acknowledging this hardship, many political leaders and media personalities appear indifferent to it. We have reached a point where a national television anchor has suggested euthanizing homeless people, while political supporters celebrate policies that continue to worsen conditions for the poor and working class. Such callousness toward human suffering should alarm every American.

At the same time, the people entrusted with managing our economy appear increasingly detached from reality. The Director of the National Economic Council has publicly boasted about Americans' credit card usage as if rising debt were a sign of prosperity. Donald Trump has suggested 50-year mortgages as a solution to housing affordability, a proposal that would saddle future generations with decades of additional debt rather than address the underlying causes of the housing crisis. These statements reflect a profound misunderstanding of the financial pressures facing working families.

Employment opportunities are becoming increasingly difficult to secure. Many job seekers report encountering misleading hiring practices, including "ghost jobs" and postings that appear designed merely to gauge the labor market rather than fill actual positions. As a result, unemployed Americans are forced to spend countless hours applying for jobs that may not even exist.

Meanwhile, large corporations continue to consolidate power across entire sectors of the economy. From media conglomerates to meat processing companies, monopolistic behavior has reduced competition, limited consumer choice, and concentrated economic power in the hands of a small number of corporations. At the same time, technology companies and billionaires are collecting and monetizing vast amounts of consumer data, enabling unprecedented surveillance of Americans' shopping habits and purchasing behavior.

Equally troubling are the growing concerns about corruption and conflicts of interest at the highest levels of government. Reports continue to emerge involving insider trading allegations connected to powerful political figures. Trump Media and World Liberty Financial have generated substantial financial gains while Donald Trump remains a central political figure, raising legitimate questions about the intersection of public office and private enrichment. Americans deserve confidence that policy decisions are being made in the public interest rather than for personal or financial benefit.

Energy policy represents another area where the interests of powerful corporations appear to take precedence over the interests of the public. Americans are repeatedly told that expanded oil production is the answer to every economic challenge, despite growing demand for diverse and sustainable energy solutions. The "drill, baby, drill" agenda continues to be promoted even when many communities oppose further development. Once again, the priorities of large oil companies are being advanced at the expense of vulnerable communities, protected lands, and long-term environmental stewardship.

Finally, the United States is increasingly isolating itself from allies and trading partners around the world. At the same time, our economy has become intertwined with speculative and largely unregulated prediction markets, creating additional avenues for manipulation and instability. These developments raise serious questions about the resilience, transparency, and long-term health of our economic system.

Taken together, these issues paint a troubling picture of a country where concentrated wealth and power continue to expand while ordinary Americans struggle to afford basic necessities. The examples below illustrate the concerns outlined above. Some are included as historical context, but all help demonstrate the broader patterns that have led to my frustration and alarm.
