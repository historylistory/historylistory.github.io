### Key Battles

The first shots were fired at Lexington and Concord on April 19, 1775, when British regulars marched to seize colonial militia supplies. The costly British victory at Bunker Hill (June 1775) proved the colonists could stand against professional soldiers. Washington's daring crossing of the Delaware River on Christmas night 1776 led to a surprise victory at Trenton that revived patriot morale. The decisive American victory at Saratoga (October 1777) convinced France to enter the war as an ally. The final major engagement came at Yorktown in 1781, where Washington's forces, supported by French troops and a French naval blockade, forced the surrender of General Cornwallis's army, effectively ending the war.

### Political Impact

The Declaration of Independence, adopted on July 4, 1776, articulated the philosophical foundations of the revolution — natural rights, government by consent, and the right of revolution. After independence, the Articles of Confederation (ratified 1781) established the first national government, but its weaknesses — no power to tax, no executive branch, no national judiciary — led to growing calls for reform. The Constitutional Convention of 1787 replaced the Articles with the Constitution, creating the framework of government that endures today.

### International Alliances

France's formal alliance with the United States in 1778, following the American victory at Saratoga, proved decisive to the war's outcome. France provided troops, naval power, military supplies, and crucial financial support. Spain and the Dutch Republic also entered the conflict against Britain, stretching British resources across multiple theaters. The Treaty of Paris (1783) formally ended the war, with Britain recognizing American independence and ceding territory east of the Mississippi River.
