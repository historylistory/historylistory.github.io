Key Battles

The war began when Confederate forces bombarded Fort Sumter in Charleston Harbor on April 12, 1861. The First Battle of Bull Run (July 1861) shattered Northern hopes for a quick victory. The Battle of Antietam (September 1862) became the bloodiest single day in American history and gave Lincoln the opportunity to issue the Emancipation Proclamation. The three-day Battle of Gettysburg (July 1863) marked the turning point of the war, ending Lee's second invasion of the North. Grant's Siege of Vicksburg, which fell on July 4, 1863, gave the Union control of the Mississippi River and split the Confederacy in two.

Political Leaders

Abraham Lincoln navigated the Union through its greatest crisis, issuing the Emancipation Proclamation on January 1, 1863, which declared enslaved people in Confederate states to be free. His Gettysburg Address redefined the war as a struggle for equality and democracy. Jefferson Davis led the Confederacy as its president, struggling to maintain unity among the seceded states. Lincoln's assassination on April 14, 1865, just days after Lee's surrender, left the enormous task of Reconstruction to his successor, Andrew Johnson.

Reconstruction

The period following the war (1865–1877) sought to reintegrate the Southern states and define the status of formerly enslaved people. The Freedmen's Bureau provided aid and education to formerly enslaved people and poor whites. Radical Republicans in Congress pushed for civil rights protections, leading to the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments. However, the Compromise of 1877 effectively ended federal Reconstruction efforts, and the rise of Jim Crow laws soon undermined many of the gains that had been made.
