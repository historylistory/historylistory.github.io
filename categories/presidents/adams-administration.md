### Cabinet

John Adams retained much of Washington's cabinet, including Timothy Pickering as Secretary of State, Oliver Wolcott Jr. as Secretary of the Treasury, and James McHenry as Secretary of War. Many of these holdovers remained loyal to Alexander Hamilton rather than Adams, creating internal friction that plagued his presidency.

### Judiciary

The Judiciary Act of 1801, passed in the final weeks of Adams's presidency, created new circuit courts and judicial positions. Adams rushed to fill these seats with Federalist judges — the so-called "midnight judges" — before Jefferson took office. This act set the stage for the landmark Supreme Court case Marbury v. Madison (1803).

### Foreign Policy

The XYZ Affair of 1797–1798 brought the United States to the brink of war with France after French agents demanded bribes from American diplomats. The resulting Quasi-War saw naval skirmishes in the Caribbean. Adams controversially chose diplomacy over full-scale war, sending a peace commission that negotiated the Convention of 1800. He also signed the Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798, which restricted immigration and criminalized criticism of the government.
