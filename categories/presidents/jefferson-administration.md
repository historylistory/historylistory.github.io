### Louisiana Purchase

In 1803, Jefferson negotiated the purchase of the Louisiana Territory from Napoleon's France for approximately $15 million, doubling the size of the United States. The deal raised constitutional questions — Jefferson himself doubted whether the president had the authority to acquire new territory — but the Senate ratified the treaty. Jefferson then commissioned the Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806) to explore the vast new territory, mapping routes to the Pacific and documenting the land, peoples, and natural resources of the West.

### Domestic Policy

Jefferson's presidency marked the first peaceful transfer of power between political parties. He reduced the size of the military, cut internal taxes (including the whiskey tax), and worked to pay down the national debt. His administration repealed the Judiciary Act of 1801 and pursued a vision of an agrarian republic with limited federal power, in contrast to the Federalist vision of a strong central government.

### Foreign Policy

Jefferson faced ongoing challenges from the Barbary States of North Africa, whose pirates demanded tribute from American merchant ships. The First Barbary War (1801–1805) saw the young U.S. Navy project force across the Atlantic. Later, escalating tensions between Britain and France led Jefferson to sign the Embargo Act of 1807, which banned all American exports in an attempt to pressure both European powers. The embargo proved deeply unpopular and economically damaging, particularly to New England merchants.
